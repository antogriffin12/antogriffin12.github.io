Mapping Mortality in Chile

Antonia Griffin | LinkedIn | GitHub | Portfolio

This is my final project for PP434: Automated Data Visualisation for Public Policy.

Aims

This project examines regional health inequalities in Chile through seven interactive visualisations covering 2002 to 2022. I quantify geographic disparities, identify their demographic and structural drivers, and assess whether gaps are narrowing or widening. Beyond crude mortality rates, I explore age at death to identify regional blue zones and demonstrate automated API-driven data collection to predict life expectancy.

1. Regional mortality landscape

Chile's south-central regions experienced 8-9 deaths per 1,000 by 2022, compared with around 5 in northern regions such as Tarapacá and Antofagasta. This 40% gap remains stable over two decades. COVID hit southern areas hardest, highlighting vulnerabilities in regions with older populations and weaker health infrastructure.

2. The gap is widening

The mortality gap remained around 2.0 deaths per 1,000 from 2002 to 2011. This period coincided with Chile's 2005 GES (Explicit Health Guarantees) reform, which provided legally binding healthcare rights for priority conditions. Although GES improved treatment access, it did not reduce regional disparities. Since 2012, the gap has widened, and COVID-19 significantly accelerated this trend, doubling inequalities to nearly 4.0 deaths per 1,000 by 2022, the highest in twenty years.

3. What are the leading causes of death?

Circulatory diseases and cancer drive most deaths in Chile, reflecting the epidemiological shift to chronic illnesses, typical in ageing populations. COVID became the third-leading cause in 2020-2021. Regional differences are notable: metropolitan zones have higher cancer mortality, while southern regions face more respiratory deaths even before COVID. These patterns highlight the need for region-specific health policies.

4. The COVID-19 shock

To assess COVID's impact on mortality, I built a linear regression model using 26 years of pre-pandemic data (1994-2019). The model (R² = 0.960) shows a steady increase of about 1,364 deaths annually as Chile's population ages. Based on this trend, Chile would have had roughly 109,000 deaths in 2020 (126,100 died), and 110,400 in 2021 (137,600 died), with excess mortality totalling 69,600 over three years (21% increase).

5. Age structure as a determinant of mortality

The Swaroop Index measures the percentage of deaths among people aged 50+ (proxy for population ageing). In Chile, it ranges from 85% in Tarapacá to 92% in Ñuble, as northern mining regions attract younger people, while southern agricultural areas have aged as youth migrate to cities.
A scatter plot shows a positive correlation between demographic composition and mortality rates, but only explains 45% of the variation (r² = 0.45). Demographics influence outcomes but do not solely determine them.

Data and construction: DEIS microdata filtered for age in years (n=108,285 in 2019). Calculated percentage of deaths in individuals aged 50+ per region. Vega-Lite scatter plot with sqrt-scaled bubble size representing population; regression layer via transform.

6. Spatial variations in lifespans

Mean age at death varies by 6.2 years across Chilean regions, with Valparaíso at 74.5 years, known as a blue zone for long-lived populations, and Tarapacá at 68.3 years. Valparaíso has high crude mortality but an older population, while Tarapacá has the opposite. Coastal regions like Valparaíso and Coquimbo consistently have the highest longevity, indicating factors beyond healthcare, such as geography, climate, or lifestyle, influence lifespan.
The bars show the interquartile range, the thick line the median, and the circle the mean age.

7. Predicting life expectancy

Chile's life expectancy rose from 77 to over 80 years between 2000 and 2019. While a predictive model trained on this pre-pandemic period (R² = 0.916, RMSE = 0.28 years) demonstrates a strong fit, the visualisation reveals a break in 2020. The red dashed prediction line continues upward, and actual life expectancy drops sharply and remains depressed through 2022. This gap quantifies COVID's demographic toll, showing how a single external shock can reverse decades of health progress. The contrast between predicted and actual trajectories illustrates the value of baseline models for measuring crisis impacts.

Data and construction: Life expectancy accessed via World Bank API (indicator: SP.DYN.LE00.IN). Machine learning model trained on 2000-2019 data. Vega-Lite pivot and fold transforms enable dual line encoding with interactive hover.

Data, Tools and Analytical Challenges

The dataset includes 2.65 million mortality records from DEIS (1990-2022) processed in Python with pandas, combined with INE population estimates to calculate regional mortality rates. Life expectancy and socioeconomic indicators were retrieved via the World Bank API.
The main technical challenge was processing millions of records. Initial attempts without low_memory=False caused type inference errors. After fixing this, I used ICD-10 to classify causes into 13 categories, including COVID-19 (U07.1, U07.2). Standardising geographic names across DEIS, INE, and GeoJSON formats was also challenging, especially for Magallanes.
All visualisations use Vega-Lite with time sliders, regression transforms, conditional encoding, and interactivity. The full workflow is reproducible via linked GitHub and Colab resources.

Conclusions

Chile's regional health inequalities are significant, with southern regions experiencing nearly double the mortality rates of northern areas. Yet longevity patterns reveal important nuance: Valparaíso achieves the highest age at death (74.5 years) while Tarapacá, despite lower mortality, shows earlier deaths (68.3 years). This difference between crude rates and actual longevity demonstrates that high mortality can reflect an aged population rather than poor health outcomes.
However, demographic ageing explains 45% of regional variation in mortality, indicating that healthcare quality and socioeconomic disparities are policy-meaningful issues. COVID exposed these structural weaknesses, with 69,600 excess deaths falling disproportionately on regions. Chile needs differentiated regional strategies: southern regions require systems optimised for ageing populations with chronic diseases, while northern areas need services tailored to younger working populations. The widening gap documented here should serve as a wake-up call for policymakers.
